Steps in Setting Up Control Charts

  1. Choose the characteristic you want to chart.

Use the following as guides:

  1. Give high priority to characteristics that are running defective and where adjustment controls are available to the worker. A Pareto analysis can be used to establish priorities.

  1. Identify the process variables and conditions contributing to end product characteristics.

  2. Choose characteristics that provide the kind of data needed to diagnose problems. Attribute data (e.g., percent defective) provides summary information, but you may need to supplement this date with variables data (e.g., numerical diameter of individual pieces) to diagnose causes and determine action.

  3. Determine the earliest point in the production process at which testing can be done to get information on assignable causes so that the chart can serve as an effective early warning device to prevent non-conformities.

  4. Choose the type of control chart.

  5. Decide the central line to use and the basis of calculating the control limits.

The central line may be the average of past data, or may be a desired average (i.e. standard value). The limits are usually set at +/- 3 sigma, but other multiples may be chosen for different statistical risks.

  1. Choose the rational subgroup.

Rational subgrouping is the selection of each subgroup from a single distinct population. If possible, the measurements within a subgroup should be taken from consecutively produced items. For example, a subgroup of consecutively produced items taken from the same die cavity of a multiple-cavity mold is appropriate. By using items from different die cavities in the same subgroup, the range is a function of cavity-to-cavity differences rather than time-to-time differences on which the charts are based. By taking all of the individuals in one subgroup from the same die-cavity but taking successive subgroups from different die cavities, the range maybe smaller but the mean is inflated by the cavity-to-cavity differences. As a general rule, a separate die cavity must be treated as a separate machine and plotted on a separate set of charts. The same logic holds for any multiple-position machines.

  1. Choose the frequency of subgroups (individuals within a subgroup should be consecutive if possible as noted above). The rate of change in the process (tool wear or deterioration of a chemical solution) will determine the maximum time to allow between subgroups. Sample a new process frequently until the data indicate the frequency can be reduced.

  2. Choose the subgroup size.

The larger the subgroup size the smaller the standard deviation of the distribution of averages, the tighter the control limits, and the more sensitive the chart for XBar. However, as the subgroup size gets larger, the time required to obtain and plot the data gets longer. VISUAL requires that subgroup size be equal for a given quality characteristic of a specified process.

  1. Provide the system for collecting data from the shop floor.

  2. Calculate control limits and analyze the results, taking corrective actions to eliminate assignable causes.
