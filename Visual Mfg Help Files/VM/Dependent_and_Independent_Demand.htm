Dependent and Independent Demand

Any demand that is the result of a customer order is considered independent demand. Any demand that is the result of an internal work order is considered dependent demand. Typically, dependent demand is induced by independent demand since you would not have any dependent demand without some kind of independent demand, however, master schedules tend to interfere with this definition.

A master schedule is a statement of supply for a given part. It overrides all measures of demand and imposes a specific quantity to be produced on or before a specific date. Master schedules are compared to some type of demand or forecast for the purpose of validating the schedule.

Because a master schedule is by definition not tied to independent demand nor the forecast, it causes dependent demand without necessarily being based on an originating independent demand.

A forecast is a statement of demand anticipated for a given part at a given time. It is an attempt to guess, hopefully accurately, at the demand for the item. If your guesses are accurate and complete then the forecast might make a good master schedule; however, this is often not the case. Forecasts do not take into account the real world limitations imposed on the manufacturing capacity, distribution capability, and logistics of the company producing the goods.

It is often desirable to make the process of recording and analyzing the forecast and master schedules as smooth as possible. This includes easy to use entry screens, the ability to copy forecasts to schedules, the ability to handle more than one forecast or schedule, etc.

The term gross requirements refers to the quantity of the item needed at a given time, without regard to whether the requirements have been satisfied in whole or in part. Net requirements is the gross requirements minus any satisfied quantity.
